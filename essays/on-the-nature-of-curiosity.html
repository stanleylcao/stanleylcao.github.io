On the Nature of Curiosity

February 2026 · English

What does it mean to be curious? It is not merely the desire to know, but the willingness to sit with not-knowing — to hold a question open like a door that leads somewhere you haven't yet mapped.

Curiosity is often framed as a virtue of the intellect, but I think it is closer to something bodily: a restlessness, a leaning-toward, the way a plant turns without thinking toward light. We don't choose to be curious any more than we choose to be hungry. The question is what we do with the appetite.

The Shape of a Question

A good question changes shape the longer you hold it. It begins as a point of confusion and slowly becomes a landscape. The mathematician Georg Cantor discovered this when he asked a seemingly simple question about infinity: are all infinities the same size? His answer — that there are infinities larger than other infinities — reshaped the foundations of mathematics.1

The key insight can be stated with surprising economy. If \(S\) is any set, then the power set \(\mathcal{P}(S)\) is strictly larger:

$$|\mathcal{P}(S)| > |S|$$

A single inequality, and yet entire worlds open within it. What began as a question about counting became a question about the nature of the infinite — and, by extension, about the limits of thought itself.

Curiosity as Practice

There is a temptation to think of curiosity as something that produces results: discoveries, papers, innovations. But perhaps the more honest account is that curiosity is its own reward — that the state of wondering is itself a kind of dwelling, a way of being at home in uncertainty.

Be patient toward all that is unsolved in your heart and try to love the questions themselves, like locked rooms and like books that are now written in a very foreign tongue.

Rainer Maria Rilke, Letters to a Young Poet

Rilke's advice is deceptively simple. To love a question is to resist the urge to close it prematurely, to let it live alongside you as a companion rather than a problem to be dispatched. This is harder than it sounds. We are trained to seek answers, to optimize, to converge. Curiosity asks us to diverge — to follow the thread even when we cannot see where it leads.

And sometimes the thread leads nowhere. Sometimes the question dissolves rather than resolves. But even then, something has changed: we have. The act of wondering leaves a mark, a subtle expansion of the space in which we think and feel and notice.

Perhaps that is enough.

  1. Cantor's diagonal argument, first published in 1891, demonstrated that the set of real numbers is uncountably infinite — that is, it cannot be put into one-to-one correspondence with the natural numbers. This result is sometimes called Cantor's theorem. ↩
